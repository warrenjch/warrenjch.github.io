# Introduction

> <i>For then she bare a son, of many shifts, blandly cunning, a robber, a cattle driver, a bringer of dreams, a watcher by night, a thief at the gates, one who was soon to show forth wonderful deeds among the deathless gods. Born with the dawning, at mid-day he played on the lyre, and in the evening he stole the cattle of far-shooting Apollon on the fourth day of the month; for on that day queenly Maia bare him.</i>

When [<u>Hermes was born</u>](https://www.theoi.com/Olympios/HermesMyths.html#Birth){: .page-link target="_blank"}, as the story goes, he decides to troll his brother Apollo. He first finds a tortoise and makes a lyre out of its shell and intestines, and then he goes over to Apollo's herd of cattle and reverses the marks on their hooves before leading them away. After he drags them all to his cave, he hides them and goes back to pretending to be a clueless infant.

After Apollo finds out, he figures out that the cattle ended up in Hermes' cave, but when he goes there confront Hermes, he couldn't get a single piece of useful information out of the infant.

So he brings Hermes to Zeus to settle the matter, and Hermes can't disobey Zeus, so he reveals where the cattle are. But at the same time, he also shows Apollo the lyre that he made earlier, and Apollo becomes so captivated by the instrument that he forgives Hermes, and in some versions of the story he gives Hermes a staff symbolizing roles connected with herding and exchange. And thus, at the end of the story, Hermes is integrated into the divine order.

Over the past three months I've been thinking a lot about the balance between grift and nihilism. In a continuation of the [<u>previous opex post</u>]({{ "/2026/01/17/dec-25-quarterly-opex.html#chaotic-outcomes" | relative_url }}){: .page-link target="_blank"}, I think many of the neuroses I notice in the AI era are a response that fall on either side of a very narrow tightrope of reasonable behaviors. Last time I said:

> Very similar people who graduate at the same time could end up in very different places in their career 10 years down the road, and motivational speakers would often sell it to you as "working 1% harder 365 days a year" or "you have to position yourself for luck" but we know that's all bullshit. Most people who want to make it work pretty hard, but some of these people make it to senior middle manager at 50 and others end up as the CEO. One didn't work 1000x as hard as the other; he simply <b>compounded more luck</b>. Sometimes, you just need to get lucky in order to get even luckier, and getting lucky is not something YOU choose. If there was a PNL attributor for life, you will notice that the largest winners and losers have the highest attribution to luck: hard work is low vol, good luck is the manifestation of a vol event. ... Respecting that there is so much randomness in our lives that we cannot be personally liable for gives us a better idea of what our limited agency can really be applied to.

In considering this it comes pretty obvious that almost nobody in the world actually thinks this way. There is this instinctive human belief in our own agency, which for most people is far overblown, and since I talked at length about why we should underestimate our own agency at length in the last post, this time I want to talk about what happens in reality when people underestimate the power of circumstance, and its implications on individuals and more importantly the group behaviors that emerge from these conditions.

As hinted above, the two reactions I observe the most are grift and nihilism. Grift is easier to talk about, and I've talked about it at length in the [<u>many</u>]({{ "/2025/09/25/sep-25-quarterly-opex.html#cheaters-cheat" | relative_url }}){: .page-link target="_blank"} [<u>times</u>]({{ "/2025/06/23/jun-25-quarterly-opex.html#ai-and-the-smell" | relative_url }}){: .page-link target="_blank"} I've explained why I hate Cluely as a concept. By extension, many of the other nonsense startups that YC and a16z back fall under the same criticisms. But it's easy for us to spot these obvious grifts that were designed to become viral; it's a lot harder to spot the grifts that are popping up around us in unseen places and infesting public discourse, and in effect impeding our ability to [<u>measure</u>](https://moontowermeta.com/measurement-not-prediction/){: .page-link target="_blank"} what is happening in the world at the time we need it the most.

In the time before dawn, when over the horizon a redness of the day to come leaches skywards, matutinal predators emerge from the shadows to hunt as the dying night-world gives birth to the new day in fitful throes. And there's a period at the start of every major destabilizing event in our world, when all power still exists in a semi-solid state, waiting to be condensed into capital, bureaucracy, or human networks, where in the shadows of the coming upheaval we can find a sudden mass flourishing of grifters and strivers.

We learned this from Hermes: guile and trickery have massively convex payouts when the relations between systems and organizations have not settled into shape.

Much in life is decided by very little - I am constantly reminded of the [<u>football field analogy</u>](https://convex-strategies.com/2025/03/17/risk-update-february-2025-rational-accounting-man/){: .page-link target="_blank"}, and it seems apt to think about this in a temporal context, when in certain periods (or "regimes") the reshuffling of power structures produces extremely convex payoffs that, once solidified, provide so much benefit that using unscrupulous methods or methods that sabotage your own social standing to attain these outcomes becomes a valid, or even dominant strategy.

This time there aren't as many personal anecdotes as last time, because I haven't been traveling and Hilary was more of the same, academics-wise, as Michaelmas. I just wish that there was more time each week to Do Things. After taking out the time needed to actually understand what's going on in the lecture notes and do the problem sheets there really aren't any long periods of time where you can just sit down for a couple of days and work on something until it's done. This, in part, is caused by a very unfortunate development in our mathematics syllabus which I will cover below.

## Math

In the first term I really only had significant issues with one topic, which was geometry. As I said, it was a badly designed course IMO and I was also told by my tutor that it was a relatively new course, which explains why it seemed so incongruent and unpolished.

This term, we had the misfortune of having to learn dynamics, which is basically just geometry but worse. This was a massive problem for me specifically, because nearly everyone who comes to Oxford to study math has done physics at the high school level before (I believe the stat was that 95%+ took Physics at the A-levels?) Not only that, 1.5 of our other courses were also physics-adjacent: Fourier series and PDEs (in which we studied the 1D heat equation, the wave equation, and Laplace's equation, which for us was the heat equation at a steady state in 2D), and multivariable calculus (yes, very unfortunately half the things we learned in calculus were physics but rehashed to use calculus methods).

So this was a massive problem because near the start of the term I had to sit down with my friend for an hour for him to explain to me the differences between force, energy, power, and work. We also had a whole argument about Newton's third law during the first dynamics lecture because I couldn't understand how the forces balanced for a person standing on the ground. I believe I learned all this before, but I have evidently forgotten everything I had known about physics after dropping it for A-levels.

It doesn't help either that a lot of what we did in dynamics built off things from geometry, because in order to calculate the trajectories of planetary motion, or to consider the rotation of axes in a moving rigid body, you have to start by considering conic sections, or orthogonal matrices, and the angular velocity in its dual forms as a matrix and a vector. Some of my friends disagree but it was the least enjoyable course I have done during the term with the PDEs part of FSPDE coming in a close second.

However I've changed my perspective about dynamics with time by a little; with the recent launch of Artemis II I've actually been quite delighted to see that some of the concepts I learned about could be applied directly towards understanding how and why the rocket was launched in the chosen path, and making sense of the trajectory from a physical sense. But as a course for assessment, it's just too much mental pain to dig through, and to reach the depth of knowledge required to truly understand or contribute to astrophysics research just seems ridiculously masochistic. I'm in awe of the people who devote their life to this work; it is truly some of the most important and fascinating aspects of science to be involved in.

Anyways, recently I was introduced to a question by a new friend I met who was insanely cracked at olympiads, and for this question he gave me a hint of how he did it and I tried to reproduce it later (which I only succeeded in after several hours - he had 3 hours to do the whole paper I think). I think the proof is extremely elegant and I reproduce it below; note that neither of the two official solutions for this question used the Vandermonde matrix, and I think this method is by far the neatest.

The question, from the Simon Marais Math Competition, reads:

First thing to notice is that we can expand the terms of $A$ using the binomial theorem:

$$A_{j,k} = \sum_{m=0}^{n} \binom{n}{m} z_{j}^{n-m} (-\omega^{k})^m$$

Then notice that when $m=n$ the term inside the summation resolves to $(-1)^{n}$, since $\omega$ is a $n$-th root of unity. Now that the expansion of the terms of $A$ have $n$ individual terms we can expand $A$ as the product of two matrices very naturally: since by the hint we want to use the Vandermonde matrix and its properties, we will express $A = BV$, where

$$V = \begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\
\omega & \omega^2 & \cdots & \omega^{n} \\
\omega^2 & \omega^4 & \cdots & \omega^{2n} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\omega^{n-1} & \omega^{2(n-1)} & \cdots & \omega^{n(n-1)}
\end{pmatrix}$$

Then clearly, we get the following form for $B$:

$$B = \begin{pmatrix}
z_1^n + (-1)^n & \binom{n}{1} z_1^{n-1} (-1)^1 & \binom{n}{2} z_1^{n-2} (-1)^2 & \cdots & \binom{n}{n-1} z_1^{1} (-1)^{n-1} \\
z_2^n + (-1)^n & \binom{n}{1} z_2^{n-1} (-1)^1 & \binom{n}{2} z_2^{n-2} (-1)^2 & \cdots & \binom{n}{n-1} z_2^{1} (-1)^{n-1} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
z_n^n + (-1)^n & \binom{n}{1} z_n^{n-1} (-1)^1 & \binom{n}{2} z_n^{n-2} (-1)^2 & \cdots & \binom{n}{n-1} z_n^{1} (-1)^{n-1}
\end{pmatrix}$$

One thing we notice is that Vandermonde matrices have nonzero determinant if each geometric progression is unique (which they are here). Therefore if $\det{A} = 0$ then it must be the case that $\det{B} = 0 $. Now the neat trick here is that this is one of the rare cases where we get to apply the slightly less frequently applied definition of the determinant, not as an expansion but as the unique function satisfying a bunch of rules regarding its rows / columns. Firstly, the determinant is linear in individual columns (we work with the columns here since they have common constant multiples). So if $\det{B} = 0$ then we can take out the (nonzero) constant factors like $\binom{n}{1} (-1)^1$, $\binom{n}{2} (-1)^2$ and so on and the resulting matrix will still have a determinant of 0. Next, the determinant is linear under sums of individual columns, so we can split the first column to get:

$$\det{B} = \det \begin{pmatrix} z_1^n & z_1^{n-1} & \cdots & z_1 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ z_n^n & z_n^{n-1} & \cdots & z_n \end{pmatrix} + \det \begin{pmatrix} (-1)^n & z_1^{n-1} & \cdots & z_1 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ (-1)^n & z_n^{n-1} & \cdots & z_n \end{pmatrix}$$

And again we use linearity, as well as the column swapping property of determinants, to get:

$$\det{B} = (z_1 z_2 \cdots z_n) \det \begin{pmatrix} z_1^{n-1} & z_1^{n-2} & \cdots & 1 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ z_n^{n-1} & z_n^{n-2} & \cdots & 1 \end{pmatrix} + (-1)^{n-1} \cdot (-1)^n \det \begin{pmatrix} z_1^{n-1} & z_1^{n-2} & \cdots & 1 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ z_n^{n-1} & z_n^{n-2} & \cdots & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

From here we notice that the matrix determinant that we can factor out here (we'll call it $M$) is the determinant of a Vandermonde matrix with distinct sequences but backwards, and that doesn't change that it is nonzero, so:

$$0 = z_1 z_2 \cdots z_n \det{M} - \det{M} \Rightarrow z_1 z_2 \cdots z_n = 1$$

Very elegant solution indeed (I don't know if the steps are exactly the same as his, but I'm assuming that they aren't too different once you split out $V$).

## Random thoughts

### Grift and nihilism

I promised that this would be the central topic of this post, but I lied and instead I will skirt vaguely around this topic, taking jabs here and there where I think there are interesting views. Really the whole idea was to write about how I viewed peoples' responses to AI and I can't in full truthfulness say that I know how to distill it down to a few key principles. Not to mention that this kind of bulletpoint reductivism is precisely what makes AI slop read like AI slop.

Instead I will look at a couple of articles that other people have written and see what points they've come up with. Firstly we have [<u>this article</u>](https://colton.dev/blog/curing-your-ai-10x-engineer-imposter-syndrome/){: .page-link target="_blank"}:

> A few months ago I went through a bit of a mental slump. I've always been confident of my abilities as an engineer, but I couldn't help but feel like my skills were falling hopelessly behind as I scrolled places like LinkedIn and Twitter. If these sources were to be believed, engineering had moved on from the medieval practice of typing code into an editor. Real engineers were now 10-100x more productive than I was. I'm writing this hoping to help others who are feeling similar anxieties.

In the earlier days (ie, a couple months back - every timeline has become extremely compressed now) you could still see very obvious fearbait online saying things like "AI is making me ship 100x as fast as before and if you don't hop on the trend right now you're getting left behind and stuck in the permanent underclass". Recently I think it's become a bit less prevalent as people begin to "solidify" their positions past the initial knee-jerk reactions towards a state of resignation or adoption.

I don't think it's important to get into the numbers of whether 10x engineers actually exist or not. For what it's worth whatever problems the author had with coding agents when he wrote this article would probably be completely irrelevant a few years down the road, and that's the timeframe most people care about: not that AI takes your job now but that it takes your job in the future, and that contributes to a higher fair realized vol of future outcomes, and therefore more desire to bid for downside tail hedges to our own life trajectories (of which there are very few or none). The last part, I think, is the source of AI anxiety, not that it will take your job in the present (if it was 100% guaranteed to do so, I reckon people would actually have LESS anxiety because everyone would know for sure that they had to pivot to doing something else, or to demand for UBI from the government, stage a revolution, etc).

Bit further down the article he points out something very important:

> I think sometimes people lose the scale of just how big a 10x improvement is. 10x is the difference between your mini-van and a record setting supersonic land jet. Imagine trying to drive your 10 minute commute down your city streets in a car that goes 600mph. Will you get to the other side of town in one tenth the time? No, because even a single 60 second stoplight will eat up your entire time budget. F1 cars slow down to mini-van speeds in basic turns. It turns out that most of any activity is not spent going at top speed.

In the same way capital is a power difference, so are the rules and frictions in which human relations are conducted. Until we hand off everything to the AI agents, there will forever be bureaucracy and human inefficiencies in a society of humans. We can take a page from modern China, in its quest to become a frictionless society. For the fallible and boundedly rational human, friction manifests as a temporal mismatch between what we have (knowledge, money, time) and what is required of us. We can oil these frictions away at a personal level through exchanging resources, but the friction continues to exist at a societal level as a macro mismatch between net demand and net supply of the various key resources used to drive power relations. So when I order takeaway in China and it comes to me in 10 minutes, I know that the illusory frictionlessness of this transaction was paid for in a sacrifice of the deliveryman's time and money (value of labor siphoned away by the platform). This is just one example, but on a national scale, the amount of deadweight loss from this mismatch is mind-boggling.

The purpose of the free market is to enable the discovery of fair values at which various resources should trade. There are plenty of reasons why it doesn't work well when used as a model to benchmark human decisions against: the resource of knowledge, for example, takes a great sacrifice of money and time to obtain and is thus the equivalent to heavy capex expenditure, which as we know has the issue of not being responsive to fast changes in external demand conditions (it also only works out in times of general stability). Another issue is regulatory capture. In some sense corruption is actually a mechanism for the reduction of productivity loss due to friction, whereas under regulatory capture the issues of friction and inefficiency are compressed and thrown upon those who have no resources with which to barter.

All this is to say: there is forever value as long as frictions continue to exist. Some of these frictions create great amounts of value, like the friction of knowledge and certification that prevents everybody from understanding and practicing the law, or the friction of throwing off accountability that drives the entire consulting industry. If the AI can't be accountable, then someone must be, and in the future I suppose coding jobs will look more and more similar to jobs in consulting companies, viz., to be a shit-umbrella.

As a sort of sneak peek at the topic of grift ahead, this article also lists 3 reasons why people would exaggerate the effectiveness of AI agents / AI-based workflows:

<ul>
<li>Good-natured folks who are mismeasuring themselves and others</li>
<li>People heavily invested, personally or financially, in the success of AI (AI startup founders, investors, etc.)</li>
<li>Bosses outright trying to make their engineers feel precarious so they don't quit, look for other jobs, or ask for raises</li>
</ul>

It's easy to fall into the trap of thinking that any suggestion of people having reasons to "shill" AI is pure copium in the face of the undeniable productivity gains it has already realized. Just to tap the sign again:

> Measurement, not prediction.

Not only is information about how exactly AI is being adopted extremely variable, individualized, and uncertain, I think that furthermore there is a necessary gap between how traditional companies adopt it and how startups, especially AI-native ones, come to use it. It's actually not very difficult to understand the difference; one is an incumbent which owns a great deal of prior output, some hyperspecific, some optimizable, and runs a much greater risk of hitting some downside tail by running AI optimization on its workflows, and the other is looking for disruption and doesn't give a shit if something breaks (there is nothing much of their own to break).

This is not a function of being AI-native, but rather the amount of IP that a company has and its size. A couple of people at the Jane Street spring week asked them about how they were using AI and the general response was that while they had access to all the best models every lab had to offer, even the ones that the public doesn't get access to, there is still a remarkable amount of work that it is unable to do because it obviously won't ever be trained on the specific IP that Jane Street has, and thus is not capable of doing the work that their traders do. And one of their presentations on central points of failure stuck with me because it's such a vol-native way of thinking, where a cascade of small errors could blow past what any individual can reasonably be responsible for, and in the framing of AI-led production it becomes clear that unless Jane Street itself gets outcompeted by some upstart AI hedge fund, which I don't expect it to be within the next 20 years, then there is plenty of room for differentiated, highly specific expertise to thrive.

(How do you manage risk in extremely complicated systems? I got a copy of Spitznagel's new book, <i>Safe Haven</i>, but I haven't started reading it yet ... I really hope I have some time to, because it doesn't look that thick.)

But back to the point, I don't think calling out measurements for being overly optimistic (for well-intentioned or nefarious reasons) is copium, because boundedly rational agents, like the CEOs of large public companies, can't speedrun the adoption of AI as it necessarily entails the relinquishing of control and knowledge in order to sweep the frictions of time and money under the rug. These frictions persist, and as the Streeters were very insistent to impart upon us, the cautionary tale of Knight Capital should serve as a reminder of the catastrophes hiding in small errors.

> Look, I'm not an AI startup hater. If you want to plug OpenAI's API into your healthcare startup I might raise an eyebrow of concern over the risks, but I'd do the same for any startup desiring to move fast and break things in the medical field. My goal here isn't to say AI startup founders or investors are evil or even dishonest. My point is to say in the droll voice of your high school Econ 101 professor, "Incentives Matter".

If your company is built on the back of AI, you are incentivized to sell AI as a miracle solution in every part of life. If you are an engineer and your boss asks you:

Executives have been claiming that everything from Agile to Meyers-Briggs have unlocked limitless productivity. There will always be a new synergistic buzzword on LinkedIn, don't let it get you down. [<u>This other article</u>](https://moontower.substack.com/p/slop){: .page-link target="_blank"} contains a link to a link from twitter that is partly a response to another twitter longform I talked about in the [<u>previous post</u>]({{ "/2026/01/17/dec-25-quarterly-opex.html#gambling-society-iv" | relative_url }}){: .page-link target="_blank"}. The part I want to focus on is:

> As a consumer of financial journalism and of writing in general, I am now at the point where I assume everything is AI and then work backwards to figure out if it’s not, based on the author and the publisher. ... If I’m reading yet another piece of financial nihilism on Substack or Twitter, it probably [is AI].

> (Kris) This slop problem is good news for legit publications like Bloomberg because at some point, many people like me will find the effort to filter out the AI-generated garbage too onerous and migrate back to properly gatekept content. Much like if the FDIC got rid of deposit insurance, everyone would put their money at JPM. It’s too much work for everyone to have to vet everything all the time. Gatekeepers have bias and risk, but they also have utility. They have fact checkers and professional writers. Decentralization is overrated.

Two things to point out:

- We underestimate the ability of humans to create slop. There's so much talk of AI slop but I think that there was already plenty of slop all around us even before generative AI was a commonplace thing ... in the sense that low effort, badly constructed things should generally be considered with as much mistrust as what we currently treat bad AI output with. From the low effort slop of yore (eg. Taboola) we've come a long way in clickbait technology. As Kris says in the article: "the adverserial attention game is more boring when you recognize it." It's precisely adversarial attention that amplifies badly deduced posteriors about the world and that's what we want to avoid as much as possible in our thinking.
- AI writing is actually still quite identifiable for now. NYT [<u>made a test</u>](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2026/03/09/business/ai-writing-quiz.html){: .page-link target="_blank"} to see if people preferred AI writing to human writing, and I must say that though I found the differences in style still decently noticeable (though in the first case I knew where the human excerpt was from so it didn't count), it's quite interesting to think that there could (and imo will) be a point in the future where I genuinely won't be able to tell or would actively prefer the AI-generated versions. I think to some extent AI hasn't been fully optimized to hack the Turing test because its optimized to be an expert on everything, and as a consequence it tends to exaggerate, hyperbolize, and generally make entire concepts and terms up to [<u>sound more authoritative</u>](https://dynomight.substack.com/p/formatting){: .page-link target="_blank"}.

Nihilism creeps up upon us because there is a grain of truth, a rather large grain of truth, at the core of the AI narrative, which has really been the narrative of human society since time immemorial: that power is the ability to appropriate resources, and that labor is a resource, capital is a resource, connections, positions, and knowledge are resources, and what we now have is a cascading supply shock in labor that strips a large group of people of their ability (or more accurately: their bargaining power) to appropriate the resources that they have. If you want to know how this ends just look at the graduates in modern China. Less than half finding full employment, many swept back into the educational system, to trade time for another chance to turn their labor into something more valuable. The price discovery here, measured in years and lives, is long and brutal and far more consequential from a humanist perspective than any market. And you might also imagine that the introduction of AI [<u>does not make Chinese youths any more optimistic</u>](https://recodechinaai.substack.com/p/ai-anxiety-is-blooming-in-china){: .page-link target="_blank"} about their futures.

It's no fun to be pointing this out but the problem with the nihilist narrative is that the people who seem to be influenced by it the most are the people who actually still have "outs". Students in Ivies, for example, or with extremely prestigious pedigrees, seem to disproportionately fret about the coming liftoff of the upper class that they see as an inevitability, and are thus trying to [<u>hypergamble</u>]({{ "/2025/06/23/jun-25-quarterly-opex.html#ai-and-the-smell" | relative_url }}){: .page-link target="_blank"} their way out of the permanent underclass by startupmaxxing. I don't feel like elaborating on why I think the liftoff narrative skips a million logical steps, but just consider for a moment that maybe, just maybe, the future isn't going to be one single yes/no, upper/lowerclass binary separation but a messy emulsion of moving and unpredictable humans as human society always has been. Then unfortunately, for everyone who wholeheartedly bought into the doomerist-accelerationist narrative, and actually acted upon it, they have to pay for it in actual vol points, ie, the shortfall between where they are at the point they realize that Anthropic can oneshot whatever bullshit AI harness/wrapper they have, and where they could have been if they didn't, you know, hypergamble from a position where the much more +EV play was to stick to a safer route.

Again I have to caveat myself with a thousand nuances here because this is in no way arguing that hypergambling is irrational for ALL of the population or that startupmaxxing is irrational for this specific overachieving subset; what I am arguing for is that 1) right tails are heavily bid and 2) if you go into a crowded trade with the intention of actually seeing it through, ie realizing the vol, and NOT trying to find the next biggest fool, then you're gonna get scalped buddy.

This brings me to the next point which is that if someone is going into this murky game with the express intention of performing GRIFT then there is something distasteful about them but at the same time I admire the wit of the people who recognize that there is an age for rigidity and lawfulness and an age for deceit and trickery, and have the balls to be the charlatan the world of overflowing liquidity wants you to be.

I can't deny that there are probably a ton of sophisticated AI founders out there who are now able to move and build a lot faster before, who are turning important academic outputs into high tech software and hardware in niche sectors that rarely ever get much attention outside of the hyperspecialized fields they are in. This is the kind of AI-native founder that really contributes to productivity gains and not [<u>this kind</u>](https://x.com/gregisenberg/status/2007563115471552894){: .page-link target="_blank"}. "[T]heir businesses are performance art for the algorithm age" - I can't imagine saying this so unabashedly with the expectation that people who read this would find it a good thing. As one reply thread points out:

> "Can you give some examples of companies/products that have evolved out of said group?"
>

### Grand strategy

In the context of the Iran war there's been a lot of talk of a "grand strategy" or some kind of 4d chess that Trump, and in some versions Netanyahu, are playing against their adversaries on the global stage. This was a point of discourse immediately surrounding the Liberation day tariffs as well - "Trump is forcing a hard landing to shrink debt", "Trump is pressuring Powell to cut", "Trump is bringing an end to dedollarization", etc etc. In these mythologies Trump or Xi or whatever other leader is always able to see 20 steps ahead with perfect acuity and anticipate not only the responses of every other stakeholder that's involved but to play their own hand in a way that strategically forces their enemies to walk into a tightening noose with every step.

If this was true, I'd argue that the balance of power would be so convincingly tilted in one way in our world that none of these machinations and shows of brinksmanship would have been necessary. You see in chess, if one side has a clear route to victory, usually there is no need to condition their victory upon a 20-step "if and only if" chain of events. Usually you're already up two bishops or a queen. There are cases where the checkmate is a lot less clear but really, with all the conflicting interests of politicians in the world and the randomness introduced by multiway scenarios, it's hard to see how a leader can hinge their hopes on plans so expansive in scope and in duration.

If anything grand strategy comes in as a crutch for lazy reasoning. And a lot of this lazy reasoning appears in the form of fitting a narrative to things, like a collective Heider-Simmel experiment where we hallucinate hidden plots where there are none, and ascribe desires to collectives of people that have no inherent desire other than the animalistic, formless, messy will to propagate. 

Perhaps all of grand strategy [<u>is just larping</u>](https://secretaryrofdefenserock.substack.com/p/there-is-no-such-thing-as-grand-strategy){: .page-link target="_blank"}.

> However, there is no such thing as grand strategy. What exists instead is a persistent desire among scholars, policymakers, and commentators to believe that states can consciously design and sustain a coherent, integrated plan linking military power, diplomacy, economics, and ideology over decades and even centuries. The appeal of the term lies precisely in its vagueness. “Grand strategy” sounds profound because it implies control over complexity, foresight over uncertainty, and unity over politics. Yet as an analytical concept, it explains remarkably little about how states actually behave. ... The further one moves from seminar rooms and closer to institutions, budgets, bureaucracies, and political incentives, <b>the harder it becomes to sustain the belief that coherence is the natural state of affairs.</b>

In other words, the idea of "grand strategy", much like utilitarianism, is a construct designed for AGI but not for messy and error-prone beings like us. This is the core issue with any argument alleging that "X action is part of a giant web of strategies to counter Chinese influence" or "Y is part of a multipronged plot to undermine alliances". If there is no straightforward first or second order effect I'm just ignoring the argument. No human, and no machine at the current state, will be able to forecast such a complex game in such a massive and crucially, VAGUE state space to any kind of accuracy.

This post does suggest a very useful alternative framework:

> A more realistic approach, then, is to focus on decision points rather than designs. Instead of asking whether a state has a grand strategy, we should ask how it resolves specific tradeoffs at specific moments. Where does it allocate marginal resources? Which risks does it accept, and which does it avoid? Which commitments does it reinforce, and which does it quietly allow to erode? These choices, taken together, tell us far more about strategy than any post hoc narrative of alignment ever could.

It is in this framework that we find an echo of the notion of "measurement not prediction", and a much more honest model of state actors that optimize for actual utility over expected utility.

### N(d1) and N(d2)

I had a couple encounters with onetouch ideas this quarter. I think it's probably because of the increasing prevalence of prediction contracts which forces me to think about how these things behave and consequently, how to measure them by the same yardstick as other instruments. There's formal pricers for this stuff like Cox-Rubinstein but as a first pass I default to the 2*delta heuristic. However if we want to be more exact, or if we are working with options that are farther OTM or have a longer time to expiry, the errors in this heuristic build up quite quickly.

The heuristic basically says: if you have an asset with spot price $S$ and a binary onetouch option with strike $K$ that pays out 1 if the spot price reaches $K$ before expiry, you can approximate the fair value of that option by taking $2 \cdot \Delta$ where $\Delta$ is the delta of the OTM option at that strike. The reasoning is basically an application of the mirroring principle; if we approximate the probability the strike $K$ option being ITM at expiry by $\Delta$ then for any path that's ITM, it had to have passed $K$ at some point on its way to the final settlement price (lazily applied intermediate value theorem spotted in the wild). If you imagine all outcome paths as equally likely (which isn't true under drift, but we assume that at small enough timeframes or when $\lvert K-S \rvert \ll S$ - this is an intuitive reason for why the approximation gets very bad when these two conditions don't hold), then for any path ending ITM there's an equal likelihood path that's flipped the first time it hits $K$ and thus ends OTM. Therefore by the approximation around twice the number of paths ending ITM is the number of paths that have touched $K$ at any point before expiry.

There's a very big assumption in this which is actually wrong:

> "if we approximate the probability the strike $K$ option being ITM at expiry by $\Delta$..."

Well we can't actually do that because $\Delta = N(d_1)$ and $P(ITM) = N(d_2)$. To see the difference we can express the EV of a call as:

$$C = e^{-rT}\mathbb{E}[(S_T - K)\mathbf{1}_{S_T > K}] = e^{-rT}\mathbb{E}[S_T \mathbf{1}_{S_T > K}] - Ke^{-rT}\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_{S_T > K}]$$

Where $\mathbf{1}$ is the indicator function and $S_T$ is the spot price at expiry. We can see pretty intuitively that this is in replicating portfolio form. $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_{S_T > K}] = N(d_2)$ is the probability of landing ITM and you need to have $Ke^{-rT}$ of cash now if you want to exercise at expiry.

Very obviously this is not the same thing as $N(d_1)$. We expand that instead as:

$$e^{-rT}\mathbb{E}[S_T \mathbf{1}_{S_T > K}] = e^{-rT}\mathbb{E}[S_T \mid S_T > K]\cdot\mathbb{P}(S_T > K)$$

Notice this is NOT the same thing as a conditional expectation, but rather the conditional expectation weighted by the probability of the condition being met, ie, an expectation taken over the distribution when the output values for states that do not meet our condition are set to 0. Then since we expressed the call in a replicating portfolio form, and the negative part is the money we need to set aside for exercise, then the first part must be the present value of how much cash we get back when we DO get to exercise. Since the payout is how much in leverage units we are long the underlying, this is just the same as $S \cdot N(d_1)$.

Rearranging this gives $N(d_1) = \frac{e^{-rT}}{S}\mathbb{E}[S_T \mid S_T > K]\cdot N(d_2)$. Notice that $\mathbb{E}[S_T \mid S_T > K] > \mathbb{E}[S_T] = S e^{rT}$ (we assume that $K>0$), then this whole thing simplifies such that for a call, $N(d_1) > N(d_2)$. By put-call parity it means that the opposite is true for puts.

The size of this difference can be quite large if you're working in OTM stuff. Empirically if the absolute value of the delta is like less than 10 this estimate is going to be off by 1.5x to 2x which is basically useless.

I'll pull up one of the really interesting ideas I came across 
[<u>from the respectable Dr. Ilia Bouchouev</u>](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1LKChhP-kSw){: .page-link target="_blank"}. Essentially, he argues that oil prices behave closer to normal than to lognormal, eg. it can go negative, which is not possible in the lognormal case, and it has strong moves in either extreme due to supply destruction or demand destruction pricing. And the implication of this is actually quite profound when considered in the context of the oil trade, because:

1) It implies that whatever IV you are using for USO, CL, oil majors with large unhedged exposures to oil prices, or any other related product is wrong if it's the same formula being applied to another asset class, because a closer model to reality would be Bachelier's model instead of Black-Scholes.

2) If the mean of the distributions are the same, then it means that OTM calls which look expensive aren't actually as expensive when you consider the real fair price, because the lognormal distribution has a fatter right tail than a normal one, and by the inverse logic of Breeden-Litzenberger it means that if MMs consider the oil distribution to be between lognormal and normal, they would set the fair IV to be above lognormal implied IV and below normal implied IV. (I realized how odd implied IV sounds) This means once translated to a price it would be more expensive than suggested by B-S... and so if your broker or data provider is using B-S to back out the IV from the price then the skew naturally looks unfairly rich.

This is why I'm swearing to never touch commodities again unless my job requires it (this is a lie, I can't stop punting).

Granted these are some of the perhaps less complicated factors, since the data was mined from accounting ratios, but for both methods only about 50% of the predictability remained out of sample. You would assume factors with a theoretical justification behind them to have much better factor persistence across samples... apparently not. This throws what I said last time about "doing math doesn't excuse you from thinking" into doubt by a little bit, but I'm willing to accept the copium that these datamined factors are themselves on the primitive end of statistically noticeable anomalies.

Next we have some nice quotes about market equilibria. There are no easy home runs. First from [<u>moontower</u>](https://moontower.substack.com/p/ubiquiti-iniquity){: .page-link target="_blank"}:

> A truly great portfolio diversifier should have a lousy standalone Sharpe ratio. If gold reliably made you money in all environments, it would be too easy to own. Instead it gets bid until you are left a collectible with mediocre (or negative) risk premiums in exchange for the metal showing up precisely when your other assets don’t.

Next a quote which I don't know the source of because I didn't write it down.

> As traders we don’t want definitive proof. If something is statistically inarguable, it will have been noticed by others and probably coming to the end of its profitable life. I’ve written the same thing in the context of choosing investment managers. I call it the Paradox of Provable Alpha – any strategy that can be quantitatively proved dominant, think Renaissance Technologies, will not take your money, so your only hope in finding superior managers is in discretionary strategies requiring a leap of qualitative faith.
